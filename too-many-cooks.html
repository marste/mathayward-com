Too many cooks

Wednesday 11th September 2013

Some 5 or 6 months ago I started working on what I thought was a pretty nice, but simple, interactive HTML5 video. Click to pause and use hotspots to find more information.

The idea was to a) showcase a service provided via well-produced video content and b) showcase the digital development skills of the company I was working for.

Although it was all but finished when I left the company a month later, it’s still not live. Too much to-ing and fro-ing between too many stakeholders has completely stalled the project.

“We need the ‘click to interact’ message to be on the screen all the time”
“The copy needs to be more sales driven”
“I’m not sure I like the music”
“The video interactivity doesn’t work on my iPhone”

etc.

These kind of comments and attention to detail are often useful in a project as it weeds out any issues there might be before throwing something out into the big wide yonder and meeting ridicule and humiliation if, or when, it doesn’t quite work. However, nearly 6 months of questioning is rather excessive on a side/fun project. But what’s the worst that can happen?

Well... while the tweaks and amends are going round and round and round again, someone could build an interactive video that’s more high-tech, more interactive and finished quicker... someone like Vincent Morisset and something like Arcade Fire’s https://www.justareflektor.com/, which, I might add, is awesome.

But that’s ok, as the video I made isn’t in the same market space as Arcade Fire, right? While that’s true, Just a Reflektor is likely to go hugely viral, meaning a pretty substantial part of the target audience for this video will have seen the cooler, slicker, quicker version. Basically, it’s missed the boat.

Solution? Wait a few (more) months and then release it, hoping nobody does something similar in the meantime. Or just scrap it.

It’s a shame, but it’s not the first time I’ve worked on a project that’s been ruined by too many stakeholders, and it won’t be the last. This is what can hold us back as developers, so when we get the chance to work with open-minded clients, who respect our knowledge, we should jump at it as that’s when we’ll produce our best work.
